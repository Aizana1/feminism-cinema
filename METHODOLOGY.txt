================================================================================
DATA SOURCES
================================================================================
Awards data
Source: https://www.oscars.org/oscars/ceremonies/<year>
The official Academy "ceremonies" pages, one static URL per ceremony year
(1929–2026). Each page lists every category with its winner and nominees.

Speeches
speech.csv — acceptance-speech transcripts per winner, with columns:
Year, Category, Film Title, Winner, Presenter, Date & Venue, Speech.

================================================================================
SCRAPING (scripts/awards_scraper.py)
================================================================================
- The oscars.org pages block plain HTTP requests (HTTP 403), so the pages are
  fetched with a real browser via Selenium (headless Chrome). The year is part
  of the URL, so the scraper simply loops over the years.
- Each page is parsed in memory with BeautifulSoup (the "View by Category"
  section). For every nomination we extract: ceremony year, category, name(s),
  film, and a winner/nominee flag (read from the field's CSS class).
- Output: data/raw/awards.csv with columns year, category, name, film, winner.
  Rows are written incrementally (one flush per year) so progress can be
  checked while the scrape runs.

Note on "year": this is the CEREMONY year (when the show was held). E.g. the
2025 ceremony honoured films released in 2024.

================================================================================
GENDER ATTRIBUTION (scripts/add_gender.py)
================================================================================
Gender is assigned per person with a hybrid, source-tagged method. Every value
is accompanied by a gender_source column recording HOW it was decided:

  category   — Acting categories are gendered by definition
               (Actor -> male, Actress -> female). Most reliable.
  wikidata   — For other people, the name is looked up on Wikidata and the
               property P21 ("sex or gender") is read. Wikidata also tells us
               whether the entity is a human (instance of Q5); studios and
               organisations are therefore recognised as non-persons.
  name-guess — Fallback when Wikidata has no match: an offline first-name
               guesser (gender-guesser). Least reliable.
  (empty)    — Non-persons (studios, countries) or undetermined.

Name handling: the "name" field is messy in older years. Before lookup we
split multiple people, drop credit labels ("Music by", "Art Direction:",
"<role> by <name>"), parentheses, trailing roles ("Producers") and name
suffixes ("Jr."), and skip organisation names (production company, pictures,
studios, etc.). Rows with several people are expanded to one row per person.

Looked-up names are cached (data/raw/gender_cache.json) to avoid repeat
queries; the cache is regenerated automatically and is not committed.

================================================================================
SPEECH ANALYSIS (scripts/analyze_speeches.py)
================================================================================
Linking speeches to gender
Rather than join two tables with different year formats, the same gender
logic (Section 4) is applied directly to the "Winner" column of speech.csv.
Output: data/speeches_with_gender.csv (every speech + gender + is_woman).

Text cleaning
- Remove the leading speaker label ("VIVIEN LEIGH:").
- Normalise punctuation/whitespace; drop empty speeches.

Lemmatisation
Speeches are lemmatised with simplemma so word forms are counted together
(women=woman, fought=fight, girls=girl, children=child). A small override
table corrects known simplemma errors (e.g. crew). Analysis uses single
words (unigrams).

Stopwords / proper-noun removal
Removed before analysis: English stopwords; conversational fillers
(just, like, say, ...); contraction fragments; and PROPER NOUNS. Proper
nouns are detected without external NLP models by (a) collecting names from
the Winner and Presenter columns and (b) flagging words that appear
capitalised mid-sentence and rarely lowercased (catches people, brands such
as HBO, and title words such as the "La" in La La Land).

Distinctive-word comparison: Fightin' Words
The two groups (women vs men) are compared with the weighted log-odds-ratio
with an informative Dirichlet prior (Monroe, Colaresi & Quinn 2008). For
each word a z-score is computed: positive = more characteristic of women,
negative = more characteristic of men; |z| > 1.96 ≈ significant at p < 0.05.
The Dirichlet prior shrinks rare words, keeping the ranking stable on a
relatively small corpus. For each term we also report the number and share
of speeches in each group that contain it (n_women/pct_women,
n_men/pct_men), which is more interpretable than the raw z-score.
Output: data/fighting_words_women_vs_men.csv.
